# Climate Finance & Risk Transfer

> Climate finance remains structurally short of stated needs, and insurers are increasingly involved through blended finance, catastrophe bonds, and parametric risk-transfer structures.

## Overview

Recent monitoring shows climate finance is moving from broad commitments toward concrete risk-transfer and capital-mobilization tools. The theme now spans sovereign catastrophe bonds, parametric insurance for emerging markets, blended finance, debt-for-climate structures, and MDB-backed resilience programs. For actuaries, climate finance is no longer peripheral: it affects insurability, sovereign resilience, and the design of public-private risk transfer.

## Key Facts

- **Scale gap remains huge:** UNDP cited an estimated **$2.4T annual climate finance gap** for developing economies, while Deloitte framed a **$1.3T/year goal by 2035** for emerging markets
- **Adaptation gap remains acute:** UNEP's 2025 Adaptation Gap framing cited on 2026 monitoring points to **$310-365B per year by 2035** for developing countries
- **UN DESA's 2026 financing report** pushes innovative financing mechanisms and cross-sector coordination as core enablers
- **Cat bonds are moving into broader sovereign use:** the ADB is preparing its first multi-country parametric cat bond for Kyrgyz Republic and Tajikistan (**$120-150M** over three years)
- **Private-market structures are widening too:** Groupama launched a **EUR120M hail-focused catastrophe bond**, while World Bank materials continue to emphasize cat bonds, derivatives, and DRFA platforms
- **Climate finance and insurance are converging in EMDEs:** UNDP- and UN-backed programs in Africa and Indonesia are pairing resilience finance with parametric or index-based insurance
- **Execution has become the new bottleneck:** COP31 coverage frames 2026 as the year climate finance must move from target-setting to implementing the **$1.3T/year** pathway
- **Quality matters as well as volume:** recent reporting continues to flag double-counting and debt-heavy climate finance structures as a policy risk
